01
Part 1
Diagnose the Repeat Purchase Problem
Addresses: Root causes of low repeat rate · Highest-leverage segments · Data gaps

The repeat purchase problem is a three-layer funnel. Each root cause only matters once the previous one is solved. Users arrive through a narrow funnel — one brand, one product, one transaction — and nothing in the current experience widens it.

Root Causes

Three root causes, ordered by leverage

Every Truemed user has already told the company what their health goal is at the moment of qualification. This is the richest first-party preference signal imaginable — collected before the first purchase — and it's currently being wasted.

01
Solvable

Users Don't Know What Truemed Is

61% of users (25,620 people) came through merchant checkout — a widget on a brand's site. They think Truemed is a payment processor. Merchant-checkout repeat rate: 9%. Direct-to-truemed.com: 22%. Referral (a friend explained it): 31%. The gap is perception, not intent — and there's zero incremental CAC to close it.

02
Structural + Solvable

No Reason or Urgency to Return

60% of first purchases land in durable categories (Exercise 34%, Sleep 18%, Light Therapy 8%) — a structural headwind. But the solvable part is equally large: savings math is invisible (HSA saves 25–35% vs brand subscription's 10–15%), there's no FSA expiration urgency, and no reorder prompt exists at all.

03
Mostly Solvable

Motivated Users Hit Friction

Even users who want to return face real friction: no visibility into what else their LMN covers, re-qualification required across merchants, reimbursement complexity (18% of one-time vs 52% of repeat buyers filed a claim), and external checkout routing. Most of these are product decisions, not structural constraints.

A
Attacks RC1

Post-Purchase Discovery System

The merchant-checkout user finishes their order on a brand's site thinking Truemed is a payment processor. Four surfaces close that gap without a new merchant integration: a qualification interstitial right after LMN approval, the order confirmation page, the reimbursement claim flow, and a delivery-timed bridging email.

Hypothesis

Close the 13-point merchant-checkout gap (9% → 12–14%) within 6 months by making the marketplace visible at the moment users are already engaged with their purchase.

Primary Metric

Post-purchase marketplace visit rate for merchant-checkout users.

Guardrail

Checkout completion rate — the interstitial must not add friction to the purchase flow. If checkout drops, pull immediately.

Why First

Scale (6x larger than any other segment), perception gap not intent gap, zero incremental CAC. Table stakes for B and C to have any traffic to convert.

B
Flywheel Bet · Attacks RC2

Consumable Reorder Flywheel

Supplements repeat at 28%. Food at 26%. These users have a natural reorder cadence — they're just going back to the original brand because there's no nudge to reorder through Truemed with pre-tax dollars. Three changes: surface a subscription option during the initial Truemed checkout for any consumable product, ensure the LMN explicitly recommends ongoing use of the supplement or product, and send a 30–60 day reorder email that links directly to a pre-filled checkout page.

Merchant Pitch

"Route your reorders through Truemed and you can charge full price while increasing conversion, because the customer is paying with pre-tax health dollars instead of after-tax income. You stop discounting. We handle the eligibility. Everyone wins."

Primary Metric

Consumable reorder rate through Truemed within 90 days (current baseline ~15–26% depending on category).

The Flywheel

More merchants → richer catalog → more users → stronger pitch to next merchant → supply grows. Each signed merchant makes the next one easier to close.

C
Attacks RC3 · Depends on A

Condition-Based Benefits Dashboard

The LMN qualifies a condition, not a product. A "metabolic health" LMN covers an entire HSA-eligible category for ~12 months — but users have zero visibility into this. Two zones: "You're already qualified" (everything the current LMN covers) and "You might also qualify for..." (adjacent conditions). Cross-category browse is currently 8% for one-time buyers vs 47% for repeat buyers — 58% of repeat buyers cross categories.

Hypothesis

Users who see a personalized, condition-organized dashboard will browse 2+ categories at significantly higher rates than the current 8% baseline for one-time buyers. Users who expand their LMN through the dashboard (adding a new condition qualification) will repeat at rates approaching the 31% referral benchmark, because they've invested in their Truemed profile in a way that mirrors the depth of understanding referral users have.

Primary Metric

Cross-category browse rate from dashboard entry point.

Dependency

Requires A to drive traffic. Without the merchant-checkout discovery layer, the dashboard sits empty waiting for users who don't know it exists.

Key Insight

The LMN as a standing benefit — not a one-time coupon — is the mental model shift that turns a 9% repeat rate into a 31% one. The dashboard makes it visible.

Part 3
Design Your Top Experiment
Addresses: MVP scope · Descoped items · Test design · Kill criteria · Scale plan

Testing Intervention B — the consumable reorder flywheel. Starting with the cheapest possible version: a single email. The goal is to validate the savings-math framing before building subscription infrastructure.

Hypothesis & Goal

Savings-math framing will lift consumable reorder rate by 7+ points

Lapsed consumable buyers (supplements + food) have a natural reorder cadence but are going back to the brand directly — often with a 10-15% subscription discount. We believe showing the HSA pre-tax savings wedge (25-35% effective discount) alongside the "your LMN already covers this" message will pull reorders back through Truemed. If validated, this becomes the foundation for subscription infrastructure investment.

What Ships in 2 Weeks

Single reorder nudge email — no new infrastructure

One email to lapsed consumable buyers (supplements + food) from the back-catalog. Contains: savings comparison (full price vs brand subscription 10-15% vs Truemed HSA 25-35%), "Your LMN already covers this," and a lightweight cross-category discovery section. No ML, no subscription rails — just copy and a pre-filled checkout link.

Why Cohort Holdout (Not A/B)

~1,330 treatment, ~330 holdout. With only 1,660 eligible users, a standard 50/50 A/B test would lack statistical power. The 80/20 cohort holdout maximizes learning velocity while maintaining a clean baseline. Grocery results readable at weeks 3-4. Supplement results at weeks 4-8 given longer reorder cadence.

Kill Criteria — Defined Upfront

No post-hoc rationalization

Kill if: <5-point lift in reorder rate OR <5% email CTR after 60 days.

If killed: The insight is that savings framing alone isn't enough — reconsider whether the friction is awareness or catalog depth. Pivot to testing Intervention A (discovery system) as the prerequisite.

If signal but below MDE: Extend observation window to 90 days before calling it. Consumable reorder cadences vary widely.

Savings bar chart
The visual bar chart is the core wedge. Brand subscription discount saves ~15%. HSA pre-tax savings at a 32% marginal rate saves ~32%. Only Truemed can make this comparison visible — and it's the single strongest reason to reorder through Truemed instead of going back to the brand.
Condition-based discovery
"Your metabolic health qualification also covers..." — category pills, not individual products. This is the Intervention A seed planted inside the Intervention B email. The LMN is condition-scoped, not product-scoped, so the framing expands what the user thinks their HSA covers.
No re-qualification
The shield badge makes the friction-remover unmissable. The #1 anxiety for returning users is "do I have to go through that qualification process again?" The answer is no — and we say it three times in the email (subtitle, badge, footer).
Why no strikethrough pricing
Previous iterations showed "$HSA Price" vs "$After-Tax Price" with a strikethrough. This implies Truemed offers a discount — it doesn't. The product costs the same. The bar chart makes the mechanic honest: you're paying with pre-tax income, so your effective cost is lower. No fake discounts.

Communicate

Slack message responding to VP of Marketing.

Message to VP Marketing

Pushback on 15%-off win-back blast

The VP of Marketing wants to blast a 15%-off win-back email to all 35,700 one-time purchasers.

To: VP of Marketing  ·  Via: Slack

I agree with [VP of Marketing's name] that we should be going after the 35,700 one-time buyer pool and improve repeat purchase rates 💡

I do want to flag a concern with sending 15% off to this population, as new customers may expect to receive this in the future to make a subsequent purchase. While this may boost initial repeat purchasing one time, I don't believe it will compound and may teach customers to wait for discounts. 61% of these people came through merchant checkout and many don't even know we have products beyond what they purchased. They think Truemed is a payment processor, not a marketplace. The discount solves a price problem, but the data suggests we have an awareness problem. Users who understand what Truemed actually is repeat at 22% (direct to site) and 31% (referral, where a friend explained the breadth). The gap isn't willingness to buy, it's that nobody ever told them their LMN covers an entire category of products, not just the one thing they purchased.

Instead of leading with a discount, we should be making people aware of the 25-35% savings they're already getting by purchasing with HSA pre-tax dollars, which beats any subscription discount or coupon we would send. The opportunity is to reframe the LMN from a one-time coupon into an ongoing benefit they can use across supplements, groceries, sleep, recovery, and more.

What I would propose is to split the cohort into three cells 👇

Cell one gets the 15% coupon

Cell two gets a condition-personalized email based on their LMN qualification: “Your metabolic health qualification covers these 47 products across 4 categories, here's what people with your condition are buying.” No discount, just discovery.

Cell three is a holdout

We can run this for 60 days and compare repeat purchase rate, revenue per user, and whether the discount cell shows higher unsubscribe or lower repeat quality over time. If the personalized email performs comparably without the margin hit, we've found something that compounds as we build out the post-purchase lifecycle 🚀
