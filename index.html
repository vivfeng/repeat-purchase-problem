01
Part 1
Diagnose the Repeat Purchase Problem

The repeat purchase problem is a three-layer funnel. Each root cause only matters once the previous one is solved. The LMN is the crown jewel — a single qualification unlocks an entire category of HSA-eligible products for ~12 months. Users experience it as a one-time coupon. The opportunity is to reframe it as an ongoing benefit.

Root Causes

Three root causes, ordered by leverage

Every Truemed user has already told the company what their health goal is at the moment of qualification. This is the richest first-party preference signal imaginable — collected before the first purchase — and it's currently being wasted.

01
Solvable

Users Don't Know What Truemed Is

61% of users (25,620 people) came through merchant checkout — a widget on a brand's site. They think Truemed is a payment processor. Merchant-checkout repeat rate: 9%. Direct-to-truemed.com: 22%. Referral (a friend explained it): 31%. The gap is perception, not intent — and there's zero incremental CAC to close it.

02
Structural + Solvable

No Reason or Urgency to Return

60% of first purchases land in durable categories (Exercise 34%, Sleep 18%, Light Therapy 8%) — a structural headwind. But the solvable part is equally large: savings math is invisible (HSA saves 25–35% vs brand subscription's 10–15%), there's no FSA expiration urgency, and no reorder prompt exists at all.

03
Mostly Solvable

Motivated Users Hit Friction

Even users who want to return face real friction: no visibility into what else their LMN covers, re-qualification required across merchants, reimbursement complexity (18% of one-time vs 52% of repeat buyers filed a claim), and external checkout routing. Most of these are product decisions, not structural constraints.

02
Part 2
Three Retention Interventions

Each intervention attacks a different root cause. A is table stakes — without awareness, B and C don't get traffic. B is the flywheel bet that compounds over time. C depends on A for traffic but converts the most motivated users.

A
Attacks RC1

Post-Purchase Discovery System

The merchant-checkout user finishes their order on a brand's site thinking Truemed is a payment processor. Four surfaces close that gap without a new merchant integration: a qualification interstitial right after LMN approval, the order confirmation page, the reimbursement claim flow, and a delivery-timed bridging email.

Hypothesis

Close the 13-point merchant-checkout gap (9% → 12–14%) within 6 months by making the marketplace visible at the moment users are already engaged with their purchase.

Primary Metric

Post-purchase marketplace visit rate for merchant-checkout users.

Guardrail

Checkout completion rate — the interstitial must not add friction to the purchase flow. If checkout drops, pull immediately.

Why First

Scale (6x larger than any other segment), perception gap not intent gap, zero incremental CAC. Table stakes for B and C to have any traffic to convert.

B
Flywheel Bet · Attacks RC2

Consumable Reorder Flywheel

Supplements repeat at 28%. Food at 26%. These users have a natural reorder cadence — they're just going back to Amazon or the original brand because there's no nudge to reorder through Truemed with pre-tax dollars. Three changes: subscription option at merchant checkout, LMN language that recommends subscription for consumables, and a 30–60 day reorder email with a pre-filled checkout link.

Merchant Pitch

"You can charge full price because your customer is paying with pre-tax health dollars instead of after-tax income." Truemed's value prop to merchants is margin preservation, not discounting.

Primary Metric

Consumable reorder rate through Truemed within 90 days (current baseline ~15–26% depending on category).

The Flywheel

More merchants → richer catalog → more users → stronger pitch to next merchant → supply grows. Each signed merchant makes the next one easier to close.

LTV Math

Supplements: $74 AOV × 3 purchases = $222 LTV, approaching Exercise equipment's $312 one-time. At scale, consumables become the LTV engine of the business.

C
Attacks RC3 · Depends on A

Condition-Based Benefits Dashboard

The LMN qualifies a condition, not a product. A "metabolic health" LMN covers an entire HSA-eligible category for ~12 months — but users have zero visibility into this. Two zones: "You're already qualified" (everything the current LMN covers) and "You might also qualify for..." (adjacent conditions). Cross-category browse is currently 8% for one-time buyers vs 47% for repeat buyers — 58% of repeat buyers cross categories.

Hypothesis

Making LMN scope visible drives cross-category browse rate meaningfully above the 8% baseline among users who reach the dashboard.

Primary Metric

Cross-category browse rate from dashboard entry point.

Dependency

Requires A to drive traffic. Without the merchant-checkout discovery layer, the dashboard sits empty waiting for users who don't know it exists.

Key Insight

The LMN as a standing benefit — not a one-time coupon — is the mental model shift that turns a 9% repeat rate into a 31% one. The dashboard makes it visible.

03
Part 3
Design Your Top Experiment

Testing Intervention B — the consumable reorder flywheel. Starting with the cheapest possible version: a single email. The goal is to validate the savings-math framing before building subscription infrastructure.

Week 1 · MVP

Single reorder nudge email — no new infrastructure

One email to lapsed consumable buyers (supplements + food) from the back-catalog. Contains: savings comparison table (full price vs brand subscription 10–15% vs Truemed HSA 25–35%), "Your LMN already covers this — no new qualification needed," and a lightweight cross-category discovery section. No ML, no subscription rails — just copy and a pre-filled checkout link.

Test Design · Cohort Holdout

~1,660 eligible users · 80/20 split

~1,330 treatment, ~330 holdout. Baseline reorder rate ~15%. MDE: 7-point lift (enough to justify subscription infrastructure investment). Grocery results readable at weeks 3–4. Supplement results at weeks 4–8 given longer reorder cadence.

Day 60 · Kill Criteria

Defined upfront, no post-hoc rationalization

Kill if: <5-point lift in reorder rate OR <5% email CTR after 60 days. If killed, the insight is that savings framing alone isn't enough — reconsider whether the friction is awareness or catalog depth.

V2 · Scale

Subscription infrastructure + LMN template language

If v1 shows signal: build subscription option at merchant checkout, update LMN template to recommend subscription for consumable categories, expand reorder email to all new consumable buyers (not just back-catalog).

V3 · Full Flywheel

Merchant-integrated HSA subscription rails

Full merchant-integrated HSA subscription infrastructure. At this point the flywheel is live: more merchants → richer consumable catalog → more reorders → stronger pitch to next merchant. The LMN becomes what it always was — a standing benefit, not a one-time coupon.

Part 4
Communicate

The VP of Marketing proposed a 15%-off "win-back" blast to all 35,700 one-time purchasers. Here's how I'd respond — acknowledging the intent while advocating for a better long-term play.

Message to VP Marketing

A different kind of win-back

Pushing back on a discount blast without burning the relationship — and proposing a way to test both approaches head-to-head.

To: VP of Marketing  ·  Via: Slack

Hey — love the instinct to go after these one-time buyers. We're totally aligned that this is the biggest LTV lever we have. 85% one-time, avg 1.19 transactions/user, median 68 days to a second purchase — the window is real and we should be in it.

My one concern with a blanket 15%-off blast: the data points to an awareness gap, not a price gap. Our $187 AOV is strong, and when I look at why repeat buyers came back, it's almost always because they learned something — what else their HSA covered, that Truemed was a marketplace, not just a payment widget. A discount risks training 35K users to wait for deals without fixing the underlying reason they didn't return.

There's also a repeat quality question. Supplements repeat at 28% and generate $222 LTV over 3 purchases. Exercise equipment does $312 in one shot and almost never repeats. A discount-driven second purchase might just be another durable — we'd be spending margin to win the wrong behavior.

What if we ran a 3-cell test for 60 days? Cell 1: your 15% coupon. Cell 2: a personalized "here's what else your LMN already covers" email with no discount, segmented by their first purchase category. Cell 3: holdout. Primary metrics: repeat purchase rate, revenue per user, and repeat category (are they buying consumables or another durable?). If the discount wins cleanly, we scale it. If discovery wins, we have the foundation for a compounding lifecycle program.

Either way we learn something instead of betting $187K in margin on a single hypothesis. Want to sync this week to design the cells together?

— Vivian
