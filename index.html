Part 1
Diagnose the Repeat Purchase Problem

Separating symptoms from root causes. What's structural vs. solvable, which segments are highest-leverage, and what data I'd want next.

Root Causes

Three root causes, not one

Low repeat purchase isn't a single problem — it's three compounding failures. Two are solvable. One is structural but can be worked around.

01
Solvable

The Identity Gap

61% of users arrive via merchant checkout and never understand Truemed as a marketplace. They see a payment processor — not a platform with 100+ brands.

02
Solvable

Post-Purchase Silence

No meaningful lifecycle after purchase. 66% of one-time buyers never open a post-purchase email. The critical 30-day window passes with zero nudges.

03
Structural

Durable Goods Trap

34% of first purchases are fitness equipment — high AOV, one-time need. But consumables have 3–4x repeat rates, creating a bridge if surfaced.

02
Part 2
Three Retention Interventions

Each intervention targets a different root cause and operates on a different time horizon. At least one is a flywheel bet that compounds over time.

01
Flywheel

Post-Purchase Cross-Category Journey

A smart lifecycle sequence triggered after first purchase that surfaces adjacent categories — turning a one-time equipment buyer into a supplements browser within 30 days.

Hypothesis

Users shown relevant cross-category products within 14 days will have 2x the repeat rate of control.

Primary Metric

60-day repeat purchase rate among targeted cohort.

Why It Compounds

Each cross-category buyer generates data that improves recommendations for the next cohort.

Trade-off

Risk of email fatigue in a health context. Guardrail: unsubscribe rate < 2%.

02
Conversion Lift

Merchant Checkout → Marketplace Bridge

Redesign the post-checkout confirmation for merchant-acquired users to educate them that Truemed is a marketplace — with a personalized "what else your HSA covers" module.

Hypothesis

Adding marketplace discovery to merchant checkout will increase 30-day return visits by 3x.

Primary Metric

30-day logged-in return rate for merchant-checkout users.

Target Segment

61% of users acquired via merchant checkout (currently 9% repeat — the lowest).

Trade-off

Merchant partners may resist redirecting attention from their brand. Requires co-branding.

03
Conversion Lift

Reimbursement as Reactivation

Turn the HSA/FSA claim flow into a product surface. Users who file claims are 2.9x more likely to buy again — use this moment to surface "more products you qualify for."

Hypothesis

Embedding recommendations in the claims flow will convert 15% of claim-filers into second-purchase browsers.

Primary Metric

Browse-to-purchase conversion within the claims flow.

Why This Moment

User is engaged, trusts the platform, and is actively thinking about their HSA balance.

Trade-off

Compliance risk — mixing financial flows with commerce. Must feel helpful, not salesy.

03
Part 3
Design Your Top Experiment

Starting with Intervention #1 — the highest-conviction, highest-compounding bet. Smallest shippable version, test design, kill criteria, and scale plan.

Week 1–2 · MVP

Ship the smallest version

A 3-email post-purchase sequence triggered at 3, 10, and 21 days. Personalized by purchase category. Descoping: no ML recommendations, no on-site personalization — just category-mapped emails.

Week 2–8 · Test

Cohort holdout, 6-week run

70/30 split of new purchasers. Holdout gets current experience (order confirmation only). MDE: 3pp lift in 60-day repeat rate. ~3,500 users per arm.

Week 8 · Evaluate

Kill criteria defined upfront

Kill if: lift < 1.5pp, or unsubscribe > 3%. Iterate if: 1.5–3pp lift. Scale if: > 3pp with clean guardrails.

V2 · Scale

From emails to product surfaces

Extend cross-category recommendations to the logged-in dashboard, claims flow, and merchant checkout confirmation. Layer in collaborative filtering.

V3 · Compound

The flywheel spins

Personalized marketplace, subscription-eligible nudges, and a referral program that educates new users about the full marketplace from day one.

Part 4
Communicate

The VP of Marketing proposed a 15%-off "win-back" blast to all 35,700 one-time purchasers. Here's how I'd respond — acknowledging the intent while advocating for a better long-term play.

Message to VP Marketing

A different kind of win-back

Pushing back on a discount blast without burning the relationship — and proposing a way to test both approaches head-to-head.

To: VP of Marketing  ·  Via: Slack

Hey — love the instinct to re-engage our one-time buyers. That 85% number is exactly what I've been digging into, and I think we're aligned that it's our biggest LTV lever.

I've been looking at the data, and I have a concern about the blanket 15%-off approach: our repeat buyers today are driven by discovery (learning what else their HSA covers), not by price. The user quotes all point to an awareness gap, not a willingness-to-pay gap. A discount blast risks training 35K users to wait for deals — and our $187 AOV is already strong, suggesting price isn't the friction.

What if we tested both? I'd propose splitting the 35.7K cohort three ways: (1) your 15%-off win-back, (2) a personalized "here's what else your HSA covers" email tailored to their purchase category, and (3) a holdout control. We measure 60-day repeat rate and revenue per user across all three. That way we get a clean read on which lever actually moves retention.

If the discount wins, we scale it. If discovery wins, we have the foundation for a compounding lifecycle program. Either way, we learn something instead of guessing.

Want to hop on a quick call this week to sketch out the test design together?

— Vivian
