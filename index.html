01
Part 1
Diagnose the Repeat Purchase Problem
Addresses: Root causes of low repeat rate · Highest-leverage segments · Data gaps

The repeat purchase problem is a three-layer funnel. Each root cause only matters once the previous one is solved. Customers arrive through a narrow funnel, one brand, one product, one transaction, and nothing in the current experience widens it.

Root Causes

Three root causes, ordered by leverage

Every Truemed customer has already told the company what their health goal is at the moment of qualification. This is the richest first-party preference signal imaginable, collected before the first purchase, and it's currently being wasted.

01
Solvable

Customers Don't Know What Truemed Is

61% of customers (25,620 people) came through merchant checkout, a widget on a brand's site. They think Truemed is a payment processor. Merchant-checkout repeat rate: 9%. Direct-to-truemed.com: 22%. Referral (a friend explained it): 31%. The gap is perception, not intent, and there's zero incremental CAC to close it.

02
Structural + Solvable

No Reason or Urgency to Return

60% of first purchases land in durable categories (Exercise 34%, Sleep 18%, Light Therapy 8%), a structural headwind. But the solvable part is equally large: savings math is invisible (HSA saves 25–35% vs brand subscription's 10–15%), there's no FSA expiration urgency, and no reorder prompt exists at all.

03
Mostly Solvable

Motivated Customers Hit Friction

Even customers who want to return face real friction: no visibility into what else their LMN covers, re-qualification required across merchants, reimbursement complexity (18% of one-time vs 52% of repeat buyers filed a claim), and external checkout routing. Most of these are product decisions, not structural constraints.

Prioritization Framework

Why these three interventions

The three interventions emerged from evaluating a much larger set of potential solutions and deliberately deprioritizing some in favor of higher-leverage plays.

What Was Considered and Deprioritized
×Scaling the referral program. Referral has 31% repeat rate, but only 5% of customers. The high rate may reflect self-selection rather than the referral mechanic. We're replicating what referrers do (context-setting, peer validation) through product surfaces rather than scaling the channel.
×FSA expiration urgency campaigns. The most natural time-based trigger in the business. Low-effort, but it's a seasonal one-time lift, not a structural fix. It becomes a component of A's v2 bridging email.
×Fixing login/account friction. It seems this has been addressed recently by the team as now login no longer requires a password but can be authenticated with one-time passcodes sent to emails.
×Leading with merchant BD for consumables. If the catalog audit reveals thin consumable coverage, this becomes the top priority — above all three interventions. We're treating it as a dependency (validated during B planning) rather than a primary lever because the data suggests the bigger gap is demand-side, not supply-side.
×Blanket discount offers. Proven to teach discount dependence without addressing root causes. The data shows the gap is awareness, not price — customers who understand what Truemed is repeat at 22–31% without any discount.
Why A, B, and C Survived

The three interventions that survived scrutiny map directly to the three-layer funnel: A addresses identity (RC1 — customers don't know what Truemed is), B addresses urgency and supply (RC2 — no reason to return), and C addresses friction (RC3 — even motivated customers face barriers). Each solves a different root cause that must be sequenced: awareness before motivation, motivation before removing friction on return. No reorder dashboard matters if customers never return. No discovery email matters if the only impression customers have is "payment processor."

This framework also reflects what's actually testable in the 6-month timeframe without requiring Truemed to rebuild from the ground up. The deprioritized ideas may become leveraged later (referral expansion if C compounds faster than expected, merchant BD if consumable catalog depth emerges as the constraint, discount testing if identity and urgency interventions plateau), but they don't address the binding constraints identified in the funnel diagnosis.

Hypothesis

Close the 13-point merchant-checkout gap (9% → 12–14%) within 6 months by making the marketplace visible at the moment customers are already engaged with their purchase.

Primary Metric

Post-purchase marketplace visit rate for merchant-checkout customers. Baseline: direct-to-truemed.com customers visit at 22%. We need to establish the current merchant-checkout visit rate (likely far lower) and expect it to increase. Secondary: 90-day repeat purchase rate for merchant-checkout customers (currently 9%, target ~13%).

Guardrail

Checkout completion rate for the qualification interstitial. Kill if completion drops more than 2 points vs. control. Email unsubscribe rate for the bridging email.

Why This Intervention

Merchant-checkout is the largest segment by 6x, the gap is a perception problem not an intent problem (these customers had a great first experience and saved money), and every repeat purchase from this cohort is at zero incremental CAC, pure margin. Without A, B and C have no traffic to convert. This is table stakes.

Target

All merchant-checkout customers (25,620 people), with content personalized by LMN condition and first-purchase category.

Behavior Change
"Truemed is how I paid for this one thing"→"Truemed is a marketplace where my HSA covers an entire category of health products."
Trade-offs & Risks
Funnel disruption (biggest risk). The qualification interstitial sits between qualifying and completing a purchase. If it feels like an upsell, it erodes trust and kills conversion. Mitigation: informational tone ("here's what you're entitled to"), not commercial. Primary CTA is always "Continue to checkout."
Merchant relationship risk. Some merchants may object to Truemed showing breadth during a flow initiated from their site. Mitigation: show category-level breadth, not specific competing merchant products.
Contractual/data risk. Need to confirm merchant contracts allow post-purchase outreach. If not, email is blocked. Lean on interstitial, confirmation page, and reimbursement flow.
Catalog thinness. If categories shown have few products, the reveal backfires. Suppress the interstitial for conditions with thin coverage.

Hypothesis

Consumable buyers who are offered a subscription option at first checkout, backed by an LMN that explicitly recommends ongoing use, will subscribe at meaningfully higher rates than the current organic reorder rate, because two barriers fall simultaneously: the clinical justification for recurring HSA purchases is pre-built into the LMN, and the reorder path is frictionless (confirm and pay, no re-qualification). The 30–60-day reorder email then recaptures customers who didn't subscribe at checkout by making the savings comparison explicit (25–35% HSA tax savings vs. 10–15% brand subscription discount).

Primary Metric

Consumable reorder rate through Truemed, the percentage of consumable first-purchasers who make a second consumable purchase through Truemed within 90 days. Baselines: Supplements & Nutrition repeat at 28% ($74 AOV). Healthy Food & Groceries repeat at 26% ($63 AOV). Most reorders currently leak to brand-direct subscriptions. The filtered lapsed cohort baseline (customers who haven't reordered yet) is ~15%.

Guardrail

First guardrail: email open rate signals interest in subscriptions of consumables and can be compared with product recommendation email open rate. Second guardrail: email fatigue through unsubscribe rate on reorder nudges.

Merchant Pitch

"Route your reorders through Truemed and you can charge full price while increasing conversion, because the customer is paying with pre-tax health dollars instead of after-tax income. You stop discounting. We handle the eligibility. Everyone wins."

Target

All consumable purchasers as their first or second transaction with Truemed. The intervention touches two moments: the first purchase (where the subscription option is presented) and the reorder window (where a frictionless re-checkout replaces full re-qualification).

Behavior Change
"I bought my supplements once through Truemed and then went back to reordering directly from the brand"→"I subscribed at checkout, my LMN covers ongoing use, and Truemed reminds me when it's time to reorder, so I never leave."
Why It's a Flywheel

More customers repeating and subscribing → stronger pitch for more merchants to integrate → richer catalog → more options to purchase from and likelihood to repeat purchase. Each merchant that joins structurally changes what the next customer can buy.

The LMN subscription unlock: If we can issue LMNs that cover subscriptions and introduce that enablement at the time of the first purchase, a customer becomes a repeat purchaser with Truemed immediately. With a proven base of recurring subscribers, we can ask more merchants to let us add the subscription button on our checkout module, even if it competes with their own subscription, because the merchant gets the full price of the product while the customer pays with HSA, which is cheaper since it's pre-tax.

Key distinction: A flywheel isn't something that just gets better over time. The test is: does customer #50,000 have a structurally different experience than customer #1,000, specifically because of what customers #1 through #49,999 did? With Intervention B, that's literally true. Customer #50,000 can buy products from merchants that only joined because earlier customers proved the demand.

How to measure compounding vs. linear: Track merchant inbound inquiry rate correlated to subscriber volume. If merchants start approaching Truemed because they see competitors getting guaranteed recurring volume, the supply flywheel is turning. If Truemed is still cold-calling brands at month 6, the flywheel isn't working. Pivot to deepening existing merchant relationships rather than expanding.

LTV Math

Supplements: $74 AOV × 3 purchases = $222 LTV, approaching Exercise equipment's $312 one-time. At scale, consumables become the LTV engine of the business.

Trade-offs & Risks
Infrastructure requirement. The subscription-at-checkout flow requires changes to Truemed's checkout UI and coordination with the telehealth provider to update LMN templates to include subscription language. The reorder email and pre-filled checkout page are lighter lifts. See Part 3 for the MVP that proves demand before requiring deeper merchant integration.
Merchant resistance. Brands may perceive Truemed as disintermediating their customer relationship. Positioning: "We're not taking your customer, we're making their reorder more likely by removing payment friction." Start with merchants who lack strong D2C subscription programs.
AOV trade-off. Lower AOV ($74 supplements vs. $312 durables), but LTV math favors it: a supplements customer who repeats 3x is worth $222, approaching the one-time Peloton buyer. Beyond 3 repeats, they're worth more.
Shopify subscription limitation. Shopify does not currently support third-party payment methods for subscriptions. When a customer toggles to subscribe on a merchant's Shopify site, Truemed's direct payment option drops off and the customer is forced into a reimbursement model (pay out of pocket, then file a claim). This is a structural platform constraint that Truemed cannot solve alone — it requires changes to the Shopify partnership. This means the subscription-at-checkout component of this intervention requires either a Shopify platform change or an alternative checkout architecture.
LMN reuse compliance matrix. Reusing a Letter of Medical Necessity across products and merchants is not straightforward. There's an unresolved compliance matrix: same category/same merchant may be acceptable, but different category/different merchant raises clinical risk (e.g., a blood pressure LMN being used to purchase products for a different condition). Clinical ops needs to define when LMN reuse is permissible. The intervention should be framed as the ideal customer flow, with the compliance constraints explicitly called out.

Hypothesis

Customers who see a personalized, condition-organized dashboard will browse 2+ categories at significantly higher rates than the current 8% baseline for one-time buyers. Customers who expand their LMN through the dashboard (adding a new condition qualification) will repeat at rates approaching the 31% referral benchmark, because they've invested in their Truemed profile in a way that mirrors the depth of understanding referral customers have.

Primary Metric

On-site engagement rate when customers visit truemed.com. Click-through rate on dashboard elements and rate of customers reaching a merchant site from the dashboard. We expect both to increase significantly from current levels. Secondary: LMN expansion rate, the percentage of dashboard customers who initiate and complete a supplemental qualification.

Guardrail

Cross-category page browsing rate (baseline: 8% of one-time buyers browse 2+ categories vs. 47% of repeat buyers). Qualification completion rate for LMN expansions. If customers start and abandon, friction is too high. Compliance review of supplemental qualification process. Additional LMNs must meet the same clinical standards as the original.

Dependency

Requires A to drive traffic. Without the merchant-checkout discovery layer, the dashboard sits empty waiting for customers who don't know it exists.

Target

All returning customers, prioritizing merchant-checkout customers who've been made marketplace-aware through Intervention A and need a useful destination when they arrive.

Behavior Change
"I qualified, I bought, there's nothing else for me here"→"Every time I log in, there's more I can do with my HSA, and the more I tell Truemed about my health goals, the more it unlocks for me."
Compounding Properties (Not a Full Flywheel)

Each customer who expands their LMN creates a richer profile, which makes their dashboard more valuable, which increases return visits, which creates more expansion opportunities. The condition-to-product matching also improves with scale. But the supply side doesn't structurally change (that's B's flywheel). Frame this as compounding customer investment, not a structural flywheel.

Key Insight

The LMN as a standing benefit, not a one-time coupon, is the mental model shift that turns a 9% repeat rate (merchant-checkout customers who think Truemed is a payment processor) into a 31% one (referral customers who had someone explain the breadth). The dashboard makes that understanding visible and self-serve.

Trade-offs & Risks
Depends on A for traffic. The dashboard is useless if nobody visits truemed.com. If A underperforms, deprioritize the dashboard and double down on email-based surfaces (B's reorder nudges).
LMN expansion needs clinical validation. Truemed can't let customers self-qualify. There's a telehealth provider in the loop. If the expansion path requires a full consultation rather than a short questionnaire, the on-ramp promise breaks. Need to understand the minimum viable clinical pathway.
Catalog depth. If a customer expands their LMN but there are only 4 products in the new condition, the dashboard feels empty at peak excitement. Suppress expansion prompts for conditions with thin coverage.
Build complexity. Heaviest engineering lift of the three. Realistic timeline: v1 (static condition page, no expansion) in 4–6 weeks. V2 (expansion flow + savings math) in month 3–4.
Consumer consent requirement. Truemed currently collects health data through the LMN qualification process solely for the purpose of issuing the letter. Using that data to power condition-based recommendations or a benefits dashboard requires explicit consumer consent — a checkbox or opt-in that says something like "I consent to Truemed using my health qualification data to recommend relevant products and health outcomes." This consent mechanism doesn't exist yet and is planned for this quarter. Without it, the dashboard can only show generic marketplace content, not personalized condition-based recommendations. The consent gate could be introduced at point of purchase, on next login, or via email ("Want a personalized health plan? Opt in here").

Referral has 31% repeat rate, but only 5% of customers. The high rate may reflect self-selection rather than the referral mechanic. We're replicating what referrers do (context-setting, peer validation) through product surfaces rather than scaling the channel. If A and C underperform, scaling referrals becomes the fallback.

Leading with Merchant BD for Consumables

If the catalog audit reveals thin consumable coverage, this becomes the top priority — above all three interventions. We're treating it as a dependency (validated during B planning) rather than a primary lever because the data suggests the bigger gap is demand-side, not supply-side. But this assumption needs validation.

FSA Expiration Urgency Campaign

The most natural time-based trigger in the business. We'd run this in parallel since it's low-effort, but it's a seasonal one-time lift, not a structural fix. It becomes a component of A's v2 bridging email.

Why C, Not B, for the Experiment

A is the clear path. B has headwinds. C is the bigger swing.

Intervention A is the clear-path play — optimizing post-purchase discovery through interstitials and emails. The team should absolutely ship it; there's less debate needed. Intervention B is the flywheel bet on consumable reorders, but it faces two headwinds that prevent a clean experiment today: Shopify doesn't support third-party payment methods for subscriptions (a structural platform constraint), and LMN reuse across products and merchants is an unresolved compliance matrix that requires clinical ops input. Both need resolution before B can be meaningfully tested.

Intervention C is the bigger swing. It targets the warmest cohort — customers who already know what Truemed is, are motivated to return, and need help finding what they came for. The core hypothesis is one nobody at Truemed has yet tested: does organizing the product experience around the customer's health condition (rather than by merchant or generic category) convert returning customers at meaningfully higher rates? If the answer is yes, it validates the entire thesis that the LMN is being underutilized as a retention asset — and it gives the team a roadmap for how the logged-in Truemed experience should evolve.

Why this sequence matters: A should ship immediately (lower risk, immediate awareness lift). B needs platform and compliance clarity before we can run a meaningful test. C represents the highest-conviction product experiment because it directly tests whether the information asymmetry — customers don't know what they're qualified for — is the binding constraint on repeat purchases among returning customers.

The Consent Gate: Experiment Entry Point

Compliance requirement, enrollment mechanism, and demand signal — all in one surface

Before customers can see a condition-personalized experience, they need to opt into Truemed using their health qualification data for product recommendations. Today, Truemed collects health data solely for issuing the LMN — using it to power a personalized experience requires explicit consent.

This consent gate isn't just a compliance requirement — it's the first surface of the experiment itself. The consent modal does three jobs simultaneously: First, it satisfies the legal and compliance requirement for data reuse. Second, it serves as the experiment enrollment mechanism — customers who opt in enter the treatment experience, while the opt-in rate itself becomes a leading metric. Third, it tests the value proposition: how many returning customers actually want a condition-organized experience? If 30%+ opt in, the concept has strong pull before we even measure downstream behavior. If under 10%, the framing needs work regardless of what happens next.

Addressing Selection Bias

Because consent is voluntary, customers who opt in are likely more motivated than those who decline. To isolate the effect of condition-framing from self-selection, we randomize within the consented population: among customers who opt in, half are shown the condition-specific landing page, half are shown the current generic experience (their consent is still captured for v2). This gives a clean A/B among customers with equal revealed motivation.

Why AI-Generated Plans Are Feasible in 2 Weeks

The v1 MVP doesn't require a production AI pipeline. We generate 5–8 wellness plan templates per condition (metabolic health, sleep, stress, etc.) using AI once, then serve the right template based on the customer's LMN condition with products dynamically inserted based on current catalog availability. Critical Language Choice

The language is social-proof-based, not eligibility-claim-based. Instead of "Your LMN covers all of these" (which implies cross-category LMN reuse that hasn't been validated by clinical ops), the framing is "People with your condition have been approved for these." Three Layers, Each Readable at a Different Speed
Layer 1 — Opt-in Rate (Days)

What percentage of returning customers who see the consent modal click "Yes, show me what I'm missing"? This is the fastest signal and the purest demand indicator. It tells us whether customers want a condition-personalized experience before we measure any downstream behavior. Target: 25%+ opt-in suggests strong concept pull. Below 10% means the framing needs rework.

Layer 2 — Post-Opt-in Engagement (Weeks)

Among customers who opt in and see the condition-specific landing page, how does their engagement compare to the same customer profile's historical behavior on the generic site? Key metrics: pages viewed per session, categories browsed, time on site, products clicked, and cross-category browse rate. Within the consented population, we also compare treatment (condition page) vs. control (generic page) to isolate the effect from selection.

Layer 3 — Repeat Purchase Conversion (30–60 Days)

Did condition-framing drive transactions? Repeat purchase rate overall, cross-category purchase rate specifically, and product category distribution of purchases (does condition-framing shift the mix toward consumables?).

Why Three Layers Matter

Even if Layer 3 is inconclusive at 60 days (purchase cycles are long), Layers 1 and 2 give a clear decision signal. "30% of returning customers opted in, and those customers browsed 2.5x more categories than the same cohort historically did on the generic site. Purchase cycle maturity hasn't been reached, but the engagement signal strongly suggests the dashboard is worth building."

Sample Size and Duration

Cohort approach (no prospective enrollment wait): From 12-month purchase history: ~42,000 unique purchasers. ~85% are one-time purchasers = ~35,700 who could theoretically return. Filter to customers who purchased 30+ days ago and have not already reordered. ~60–70% meet criteria = ~21,000–25,000 eligible. Assume ~15–20% return to the site within a 60-day window = ~3,200–5,000 encounter the consent modal.

With a 25–30% opt-in rate: ~800–1,500 consented customers. Randomly assign 50/50 = ~400–750 per group.

Power: With a baseline cross-category browse rate of ~8%, this sample is powered to detect a ~6–8 point lift (8% to 14–16%) at 80% power. A larger effect size than a single email test, but appropriate — the condition-specific landing page is a much more intensive intervention, so we should expect a larger effect if the hypothesis is correct.

Decision Framework, Defined Upfront

No post-hoc rationalization

Kill if: After 60 days the opt-in rate is below 10% (insufficient demand signal) AND the treatment group shows less than a 3-point lift in cross-category browse rate vs. control. The condition-framing hypothesis isn't strong enough to warrant building the full dashboard. Deprioritize C and double down on A and B.

Iterate if: Opt-in is strong (25%+) but browse and purchase rates are flat in treatment vs. control. Customers want the concept but the execution needs work. Try adding savings math ("Save $18–26/month on supplements vs. after-tax"), balance visibility ("You have $340 in unused HSA benefits"), or richer AI-generated plans before pivoting. Also test different modal copy — the curiosity framing may need adjustment for different condition cohorts.

Scale if: Opt-in exceeds 25%, cross-category browse lifts 5+ points in treatment vs. control, and repeat purchase rates show directional improvement. Move to v2. The condition-framed landing page becomes the default logged-in experience, and we greenlight full dashboard development.
